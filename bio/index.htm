1983
   
 
   
 
Robotics
 
My interest in robotics began in 1978 with Sherbot, where for the first time I combined my hobbies of electronics, computers, and metal working. While attending college, I built this robot on a shoe string budget on a workbench in my bedroom. Sherbot featured two tank-like tracks and a hand-built Z80 computer. It used four ultrasonic sensors along with the A* algorithm to do obstacle avoidance and path planning.
 
In the early 90's, I donated Sherbot to the Boston Computer Museum. I see now that it has made its way to the Computer History Museum in Mountain View, California. See their archive photos here: Sherbot

The Personal Computer
 
When the IBM PC was first released in 1981, I was greatly inspired by the possibilities of a small computer having what I believed would be a well adopted operating system and hardware platform. It was also the beginning of my interest in user interface design. I borrowed $3000 from my mom to buy a PC, then immediately started writing a text editor for programmers. Coded in assembly language, it was extremely fast, compact, and had a very intuitive WISWIG interface. Once completed I licensed it first to Peter Norton Computing, then Symantec. The royalties from The Norton Editor paid for my first house.

Automation in Biotech
 
My interest in robotics continued, but this time with a software only project. It occurred to me that biotech was a great place for robotics. This was just as the Human Genome Project was starting to ramp up. My first biotech project was the development of a graphic user interface for a pipetting robot made by Beckman Instruments. I did this on my own, with the hope of licensing it to Beckman. They did not see the light, instead favoring their buggy DOS application until the product was obsolete. On this venture, I think I broke about even, selling individual copies to Abbott Labs, DuPont, Los Alamos, Smithkline Beecham and Merck.

Biotech Startup
 
In 1994 I was ready to turn in my entrepreneurial hat and get a real job. As my interest in biotechnology and automation grew, I landed a job at Millennium Pharmaceuticals, a biotech startup in Boston. There I developed software for pipetting robots used to prepare DNA for sequencing. I also built several custom robots for spotting DNA onto micro arrays. During my five years at Millennium, the company grew from 70 employees to almost a thousand. At that point, it was time for me to go, but I left with enough stock options to purchase my second house.
 
The robot shown here, Zeus, is one that I set up at Millennium. It spotted thousands of DNA samples onto large nylon membranes. It was featured in the January 15, 2001 issue of Time Magazine. Read the article here.

Circuit Boards for Everyone
 
ExpressPCB is a company that I started in the closet of my Cambridge condo during the late 90s. It kept me occupied (and well fed) for 18 years.
 
Before ExpressPCB, prototyping with PCBs was not practical for two reasons: poor software choices for designing simple boards and the high cost of having them made. The first step in solving this was to develop a very user friendly CAD program, then giving it away. Next was to figure out how to economically make PCBs in small quantities. The solution was to combine the designs of dozens of customers onto a single panel, thus amortizing the setup cost.
 
While the income continued to be impressive, in 2015 I sold ExpressPCB as my heart was no longer engaged. To this day it remains a major player in the world of PCB prototyping. Check it out at: www.expresspcb.com

Back To Electronics
 
This is an under sea instrument that I designed and built for Libe Washburn, a marine geologist at UCSB. It is used to record the height of waves by sitting on the ocean floor and measuring the water pressure above. The device is sensitive enough to record tidal changes, along with the effects of waves at the water's surface. It records the wave height at a rate of four samples per sec for periods up to 6 months.

CAD to CAM to CNC
 
In 2007, I added SolidWorks to my engineering toolbox. SolidWorks has significantly increased the complexity of mechanisms I can design. Soon I became intrigued with the process of going from a CAD model to an actual part. To play with that, I bought a small CNC mill for my home shop. The mill was purchased without electronics, favoring building my own. Now I am able to go from an idea to an actual part in just a few hours. I've used the mill to make everything from robotic components to iPod stands to theatrical props.

Light & Kinetic Sculptures
 
In addition to mentoring robotics at the high school Engineering Academy, I also worked on their curriculum. Their program teaches the integration of art and technology.
 
Sophomores build a Light Sculpture, customizing to make it their own. The year long project teaches students Solidworks, machining and programming in C. Here is a short video showing their final work: Light Sculpture
 
The junior year project is a Kinetic Sculpture. The students use machining (mill and lathe), Solidworks (including a motion study) and dabble in the physics of light/color and ultrasonics. See what they made: Kinetic Sculpture
 
Supporting the curriculum, I designed the electronics and built mechanical prototypes for both of these projects, each one having an embedded Arduino computer. I was tasked with programming the Kinetic Sculpture, as it was too complex for students having numerous interrupt service routines and closed loop motion control.

Carousel Of Physics
 
For the senior project, the Engineering Academy wanted to invent something original and splashy. Built from scratch we created the Carousel Of Physics. While the curriculum was centered around mechatronics and physics, the finished product was intended to be a kinetic sculpture.
 
Constructed in 15 pie shaped sectors, each sector supports four projects. Each project was designed and built by a student with guidance from an adult mentor. The projects demonstrate concepts in physics in entertaining ways related to light, sound, electricity, magnetism...
 
I worked on this for almost a year, focusing on the electronics and coding the majority of the software, including networking the 38 Arudino processors. I also mentored several students in their design and construction.

2016
 
 
   
 
   
 
Linux
 
For decades I have been building projects using 8-bit single chip processors from Intel, Microchip and Atmel. This year I added 32 bit processors running Linux to my toolbox.
 
In this effort, I have been evaluating two single board computers: the Raspberry Pi and the Beaglebone Black. Both are inexpensive, include fast processors, have gobs of memory, and hardware floating point.
 
This website (that you're reading now) is being hosted on a Beaglebone in my house. That's it in my right hand.
