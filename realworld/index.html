ENGR3499B
Microcontrollers for the Real World

This class emulates the work that you might do as an electrical engineer in the real world. You are given the physical portion of a new product. Your job is to complete the product by developing its electronics, pcb and software.

Class description:


In this course, students will learn to design a real-world controller using modular components. The class will focus on all aspects of integrating a microcontroller with a simple product. Each student will be given an electro-mechanical device that could be a future product. The student's task is the engineering of the product's electronics, printed circuit board, user interface and software. Students will learn and use schematic capture, PCB layout, and C programming in the development of their controller.

The style of instruction will be similar to mentorship (rather than lectures or group projects) with weekly one-on-one meetings for progress reports, discussions and advising. Students will work independently and can expect a significant workload with weekly deliverables.

Overview:

You just got a job at the startup Kitty Cat Corp. Soon they will be launching a new product, an automated cat feeder, which they call the Kitty Caddy. They've already designed the product's mechanism, but still don't have any electronics to drive it. Fortunately, you got the job!  

Since the product's design is well underway, you have constraints that will influence your work. For example, Kitty Cat Corp has already picked the type of motor (a stepper) and a homing sensor (optical). The PCB's maximum size and how it mounts are set by the packaging. Choices for the UI's hardware are also constrained by cutouts in the enclosure to use a small display and 3 push buttons. But beyond that, you will make the design decisions, picking the components, creating the PCB and developing the code.

This is the device that you will receive as part of the class. By the end of the semester, you will turn it into a fully functional "product".

A significant portion of the class involves learning to use KiCad for drawing schematics and PCB design. KiCad is free and runs on Windows, macOS and Linux (Windows recommended). The design of the electronics is simplified with the use of modules (such as an Arduino Nano) instead of discrete components. This allows you to focus on creating a robust circuit board. Students wishing to work at an accelerated pace can build a second version of their PCB, replacing some of the modules with surface mount components (but this is not expected or required to do well in the class).

For code development, you will use the Arduino IDE. Because software is such an important part of modern products, it will be in this class. You will be graded on the quality of your code and the user interface you create. The UI should include all the functionality that this product needs, as-well-as making it simple and intuitive to use. 

In many college courses, student projects are of a "prototype" quality. That is not the goal here. You will design and build a controller that is reliable and robust enough to operate a real-world device. The finished board should work well and be beautiful.

The class's final deliverable is a document of your work that can be included in your portfolio. It can be formatted as a PDF or uploaded to your personal website. At a minimum it must include: 

  • A title, summary and detailed description of your project.
  • Visuals: video, photographs, schematic and PCB layout artwork.
  • C source code.
  • Parts list with costs and suppliers.

Assignment: Your First PCB:

Before designing your controller board, you will need to learn KiCad for drawing schematics and PCBs. The handout below describes the first assignment: how to design a simple circuit board. You have about 3 weeks to complete it. Feel free to go faster if you want more time for your project. Grading will be done once you have finished all steps, however be prepared to discuss your progress each week.

Simultaneously while working on this assignment, you will also complete the top-level design of your controller. This includes a block diagram of your circuit and a flowchart for its UI (neat sketches by hand are fine for both). As you design your controller, keep your notes and sketches. Employers love seeing your thought process, so these items are great material for your portfolio.

Learning Objectives:

At the end of this course, students will be able to: 

  • Define requirements and technical specifications for an engineered solution.
  • Balance trade-offs and use informed choices when making design decisions.
  • Use modern engineering tools in taking a technical specification through the design and implementation process.
  • Convey design ideas and solutions through writing and graphical communication.
  • Use an iterative process to revise and improve new designs.
  • Assess and select appropriate components in designing control circuitry of moderate complexity.
  • Consider all aspects of printed circuit board design including electrical, power, interconnection and physical mounting.
