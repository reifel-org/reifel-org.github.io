ENGR3499: Microcontrollers for the Real World

Over the summer I've been thinking about how to make a project-based hands-on class work really well with remote instruction. This class has been designed to do that. Instead of lectures or group projects, the style of instruction will be more similar to mentorship, with discussions and advising one-on-one by video.

Class description:

In this course, students will learn to design and build a real-world controller using modular components. The class will focus on the integration of a microcontroller with special-purpose modules (such as power regulation, motion control, graphical display, audio generation, digital radio communication, environmental sensing) along with the software needed to control them. Each student will learn and use schematic capture, printed circuit board layout, and C programming in the construction of a controller board having the functionality of their choice. Students will choose one additional area of study to apply to their project: mechanical packaging, user interface design, or real-time control. This hands-on course is intended to work equally well for students studying on campus or remotely. All instruction will be one-on-one via video link and allow some customization based on individual student interests, background, and major.

Overview:

You will design and build a controller having the features of your choosing. You might want to make an XY plotter board. Or perhaps a GPS tracker, or a temperature data logger, or an FM radio with digital tuning. It could be lipo battery powered or plug into the wall. It might have a touchscreen display or a Bluetooth interface. MEs will be encouraged to build something involving motion control.

To start with, you will choose a microcontroller such as an Arduino Nano or a Teensy. Next, you'll give your controller functionality by adding several modular boards. My board above has two stepper motor drivers, an accelerometer, a step-down voltage regulator, and on the back a touch screen display.

A significant portion of the class will involve learning to use KiCad for drawing schematics and PCB design.  KiCAD is free and runs on Windows, macOS and Linux. The electrical design for your project is pretty easy since the heavy lifting is done by the modules. This allows you to focus on designing a robust circuit board.

Every project will require some C coding using the Arduino IDE that you already learned in POE. At a minimum, you will need to write a software application that demonstrates all of your modules are working.  Those with a strong background in programming will be encouraged to dive deeper into the software.

Students will choose one additional area of study to apply to their project: mechanical packaging (such as 3D printing an enclosure for their controller), user interface design (how to make your project intuitive to use), or real-time control.

In many college classes, students create a project that is of "prototype" quality. That is not the goal of the class. Here you will design and build a controller board that is reliable and robust enough to operate a real-world device (even if that device doesn't actually exist). The finished board should work well and be beautiful. The final deliverable of the class is a document of your project that can included in your portfolio.  It can be formatted as a PDF or as a website.  At a minimum it must include: 

  • A title, summary and detailed description of your project.
  • Visuals: photographs, schematics and PCB layout artwork.
  • C source code.
  • Parts list with costs and suppliers.

Prerequisites, requirements and other information:

ENGR2110 Principles of Engineering is a prerequisite. 

Students are required to own their hand tools. The list of tools is a subset of those needed for POE. Remote students will also need a soldering iron.

The student and instructor will meet one-on-one weekly for an hour or so by Zoom. Before the first day of instruction, we will choose a weekly slot that is mutually convenient (I am in California, so slots will be M-F, noon to 5P Eastern). During each meeting, we will review what you have done during the previous week, introduce new topics, then make a plan for the week to follow. 

Students will have a budget of $130 to purchase modules and other electronic components.

Learning Objectives:

At the end of this course, students will be able to: 

  • Define requirements and technical specifications for an engineered solution.
  • Balance trade-offs and use informed choices when making design decisions.
  • Use modern engineering tools in taking a technical specification through the design and implementation process.
  • Convey design ideas and solutions through writing and graphical communication.
  • Use an iterative process to revise and improve new designs.
  • Assess and select appropriate components in designing control circuitry of moderate complexity.
  • Consider all aspects of printed circuit board design including electrical, power, interconnection and physical mounting.
